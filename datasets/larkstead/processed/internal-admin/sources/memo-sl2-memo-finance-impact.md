## Content

To: Mara Voss
From: Ana Petrova
Date: 2026-01-26
Subject: Holiday returns window, finance impact

If we measure the exception period as orders delivered 2025-11-03
through 2026-01-11, then 1286 orders fall inside it, and 41 returns
have been opened against those orders so far.

That is a 3.19% return rate (41 of 1286). If the 30-day window had held
instead, the rate would read 2.49% (32 of 1286). The difference is 9
returns that landed in the day-31-to-45 band, the band the old policy
would have refused outright.

Merchandise value on those 9 returns comes to 1742.55. Of that, 3 units
were opened and non-defective, so restocking fees withhold 86.70 (10%
of 867.00 across the 3). Net incremental refunds: 1655.85.

If the 45-day window had been in force earlier, HD-2024-02311 (38 days,
Ruben Silva) and HD-2025-01592 (35 days, Owen Gallagher) would both have
been approved instead of denied. Neither customer ever reopened a
ticket to ask again.

One gap I have not closed: outbound return freight is not netted out of
that 1655.85. Call it roughly 1650 all-in; I did not pull the freight
lines for this one, and if that turns out to matter later, I can go
back for them.

If 1655.85 over a peak quarter is what the extra 15 days cost, then
that is below what one escalated chargeback dispute runs us in handling
time alone. That comparison holds even before the freight gap above is
closed, since freight on 9 returns would not move the total anywhere
near a single dispute's cost. No objection from me to making the window
permanent.

Ana
