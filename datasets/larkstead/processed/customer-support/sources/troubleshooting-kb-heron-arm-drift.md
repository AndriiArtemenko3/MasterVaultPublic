# Kb Heron Arm Drift

## Symptom

The monitor slides down on its own over a few hours of use, or drops immediately after being repositioned, instead of holding at the height it was set to.

## Before you start

Ask for the monitor's weight in pounds, usually printed on the box or the spec sheet, and whether one arm or both are drifting on a dual setup.

## Steps

1. Find the tension screw on the back of the arm's vertical column. The 4mm hex key that shipped in the box fits it.
2. Turn clockwise in quarter turns to add tension, one quarter turn at a time, testing the drift after each turn rather than cranking several turns at once and losing track of where you started.
3. Match turns to weight as a starting point: under 15 lbs needs roughly 2 turns past the factory setting, 15 to 22 lbs needs 4 to 6 turns, and anything heavier than 22 lbs is past what a single arm is rated to hold and needs a support brace instead of more tension.
4. Test by moving the monitor to the top of its range and letting go. Expected result: it holds within an inch of that spot for at least 5 minutes.
5. Stop turning once you hit maximum safe tension for the monitor's weight. Overtightening past that point strips the screw and buys no extra hold.

## If the steps do not fix it

A monitor within its rated weight that still drifts at maximum safe tension usually means the internal spring inside the arm has weakened, and the tension screw can't compensate for that on its own. That's a case for the standard 1-year warranty, effective 2024-01-15, and the fix is a full arm replacement, since the tension mechanism isn't sold as a separate part the way the clamp kit is (LS-ACC-013). Rule out the desk clamp itself first, though: a loose clamp looks exactly like arm drift from across the room, and re-tightening the clamp bolt takes thirty seconds against a full arm swap.
