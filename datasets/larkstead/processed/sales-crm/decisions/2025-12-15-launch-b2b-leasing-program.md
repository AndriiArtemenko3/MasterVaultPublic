## Question

Should Larkstead launch its own per-seat leasing option for B2B accounts now that ErgoNest offers one?

## Options

**Option A: Do nothing and compete on the existing volume-tier discount alone.**
No new operational or financing complexity, but cedes the capital-free pitch entirely to ErgoNest on every deal where an account is weighing cash outlay.

**Option B: Launch a matching per-seat lease program.**
Removes the capital objection directly, but adds financing, end-of-lease logistics, and a break-fix liability Larkstead does not currently carry on any product line.

**Option C: Offer a limited pilot lease on one account tier only, sized to test demand before building full financing infrastructure.**
Lower build cost than a full launch, and produces real usage data before committing to end-of-lease terms at scale.

## Evidence

- ErgoNest's per-seat leasing option is explicitly aimed at B2B accounts and is now on their homepage as of a December 2025 competitor-note revision (competitor-note-competitor-note-ergonest-leasing-te-b4bfa6f0-04, -03).
- ErgoNest still runs a 10-18% discount band on outright purchase alongside the lease, so leasing is an addition to their offer, not a replacement for it (competitor-note-competitor-note-ergonest-leasing-te-b4bfa6f0-05). The lease itself is not a discount on their existing pricing structure (competitor-note-competitor-note-ergonest-leasing-te-b4bfa6f0-06).
- The core appeal is capital, not price: leasing removes the upfront capital ask for prospects (competitor-note-competitor-note-ergonest-leasing-te-b4bfa6f0-08).
- ErgoNest's offer has a real weak point: what happens at end-of-lease is unclear even to their own sales contact, and a leased chair that breaks stays ErgoNest's problem to escalate (competitor-note-competitor-note-ergonest-leasing-te-b4bfa6f0-09, -10).
- A live prospect quote shows the real numbers Larkstead is competing against: 42.00 per seat per month on a 36-month term, 15,120.00 total over three years plus fees (call-transcript-call-transcript-kestrel-grace-liu-l-7bd6dbc1-03, -06).
- The same account sat below both Larkstead's volume-discount tiers at ten seats, so the comparison came down to list price versus a lease with no discount lever available on Larkstead's side (objection-note-objection-note-kestrel-lease-comparison-07, -09).
- The competitive pressure is not hypothetical: Foxglove, a churn-risk account, is actively weighing an ErgoNest quote against its Larkstead renewal, due by August (segment-qbr-analysis-qbr-q1-2026-b2b-35pct-churn-risk-06).

## Criteria

Size of the pipeline segment currently lost or stalled specifically on the capital objection, cost of standing up lease financing and end-of-lease logistics, and whether Larkstead can beat ErgoNest's unclear end-of-lease terms as a point of differentiation rather than just matching the offer.

## Recommendation

Option C. The Foxglove renewal at risk and the Kestrel quote comparison both show the capital objection is live and costing pipeline today, but building full lease infrastructure before testing demand risks over-investing in a feature ErgoNest itself has not fully worked out. A single-tier pilot answers the objection on the accounts where it is already surfacing without committing to the harder problem of what happens when a leased unit breaks or a term ends.

## What would change my mind

If the pilot tier shows leasing does not move close rates on capital-constrained accounts, or if end-of-lease logistics cost more to run cleanly than the discount tier already gives up, Option A becomes the better long-run answer.

## Next action

Scope a single-tier pilot lease against the accounts currently stalled on capital, starting with Foxglove given its August renewal deadline, and define end-of-lease terms explicitly rather than leaving them as unclear as ErgoNest's.
