## Question

Now that the roster's at twelve, does first-order year predict how fast someone reorders, and what do they reorder with?

## Table

| First-order year | Customers | n | Avg. days to first reorder | Reorder led with an accessory |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2024 | Nora Feld, Marcus Bell, Ivy Chen, Ruben Silva, Tessa Nakamura, Owen Gallagher, Freya Lindholm, Desmond Cole | 8 | 187 | 6 of 8 |
| 2025 | Alma Reyes, Kit Donovan, Beatrix Mwangi, Sam Whitfield | 4 | 134 | 3 of 4 |

## Findings

1. All twelve names reordered inside a year of their first purchase, and nine of the twelve led that second order with something small: a Sparrow cable kit, an Alder mat, a Finch footrest, a Wren stand, never another desk or chair. Repeat isn't buying the big-ticket item twice. It's buying the parts-shelf stuff that wears out, or that they wished they'd grabbed the first time around.
2. Tessa Nakamura and Sam Whitfield are the two accessibility-segment names on the full roster, and both reordered inside 90 days of their first purchase. Back in the February 2025 cut, Tessa was the whole segment on her own with a clean service record; Sam joining her a year later with the same fast-reorder pattern is the second data point I wanted before saying anything about accessibility buyers in a campaign brief.
3. The 2024 cohort's average gap to reorder, 187 days, runs longer than the 2025 cohort's 134 days. Read that as Mailloft's flows finally landing on time rather than customers loving us faster this year, because the 2025 cohort is half the size and one fast reorderer moves the average by a lot.
4. Desmond Cole's first order, 2024-12-09, reads like a holiday gift: a desk and a mat billed to his own address in the run-up to Christmas. His reorder five months later was a Wren stand, which says the gift-buyer tag undersells him. He's using the thing himself now.

## Caveats

n=12, and reorder timing comes from order notes typed by hand, not a system field, so a return-and-replace could look identical to a genuine reorder in this data. The 2025 cohort's 134-day average rests on four people; one slow reorderer next quarter would push it past the 2024 figure.
